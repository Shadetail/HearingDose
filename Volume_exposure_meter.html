Instrument setup and assumptions

Two inputs, not one. The old single anchor is now split into the two things that actually set your level. The chain ceiling (119 dB) is a full-scale tone at max volume - pure hardware, from the D1's 115 dB/V and the iFi's 1.59 V. The source sets how far your content sits below that ceiling. A full-scale sine reads about -3 LUFS, so content at L LUFS lands at 119 + (L + 3) at max. YouTube Music caps loud tracks near -7, giving 115; Spotify's -14 gives 108. Then SPL = that + Windows dB.

Why the source matters so much. YouTube Music is about 7 dB hotter than Spotify, and 7 dB on a 3 dB exchange rate is roughly five times the dose at the same slider position. Its -7 is a cap on the loudest masters rather than a target that lifts quiet ones, so quieter tracks play below this - which means the estimate errs on the safe side for them. Pick the source you actually use.

Uncertainty and weighting. The whole estimate carries about plus or minus 3 dB from spec tolerance and the loudness model - fine for dose, where 3 dB is the gap between 8h and 4h. The SPL number is flat / 1 kHz-referenced; NIOSH dose is A-weighted. Typical bass-carrying music A-weights 3 to 6 dB lower, so the default flat setting over-warns on purpose. The -4 dB option is a best guess for ordinary music; leave it flat for the safe ceiling.

The model, and its limits. NIOSH: 85 dBA = 8h, 3 dB exchange - every +3 dBA halves the time; below 85 dBA there's no practical daily limit. Dose is per day and accumulates: two 45-minute sessions count as 90 minutes. This is an estimate for one chain, not a measurement of your ear, and it says nothing about a single master's peaks. If you ever pin an absolute level with a calibrated mic, set the chain ceiling to match and everything follows. With pre-existing tinnitus you have less margin - stay conservative and round down.
